Riding together

March 2026

Group riding is one of the best things about cycling. It is also one of the things that goes wrong fastest when people are not on the same page. These are the expectations for Ours CC rides. They exist to keep everyone safe and to make the experience better for the whole group.

Read through before your first ride. Most of this becomes second nature after a couple of Saturdays.

Hold your line

Predictability keeps the group safe. Ride steady and straight. Avoid sudden braking or swerving. If you need to move out around something, do it gradually and signal clearly. The rider behind you is trusting that you will not make unexpected moves.

Hands on the bars

When you are in the group, keep both hands on the hoods or the drops. This means you can brake quickly if you need to. Cover the brakes on descents and anytime the group is close together. No aero bars on group rides. Save those for solo time trials.

No headphones

You need to hear calls from the riders around you. Warnings about cars, hazards, stopping, slowing. A single earpiece might feel harmless but it changes your reaction time and your awareness of what is happening in the bunch. Leave them at home.

Call and signal

Front riders are the eyes of the group. If you are near the front, point out hazards (potholes, glass, gravel, parked cars) and call them out. Use your hand on the side the hazard is on. Every rider relays the signal back through the group. The main calls you will hear: "car back" (vehicle approaching from behind), "slowing," "stopping," "hole," "gravel," and "single" (move to single file). If you are not sure about a call, ask after the ride. Nobody minds explaining.

Two abreast, single in traffic

We ride two abreast on quiet roads because it keeps the group compact and makes conversation possible. When the road narrows or traffic builds, the front will call "single" and we tuck into single file. Move back to two abreast when the front calls it. Ride handlebar to handlebar with the person next to you. Do not half-wheel (creeping half a wheel ahead of your partner), because it makes the whole group speed up without meaning to.

Watch your wheel

Never overlap your front wheel with the rear wheel of the rider ahead of you. If they shift sideways even slightly, wheel contact will bring you down. Keep a comfortable gap and sit just to one side. On climbs, increase the gap because riders standing out of the saddle can roll back unexpectedly.

Regroup and wait

We regroup at the top of climbs, after turns, and at intersections. If the group splits at a traffic light, the front eases up and waits. Nobody should have to chase. If you are feeling strong, great. Use the regroup to recover and enjoy the company. If you are struggling, the group will wait.

Traffic lights and intersections

Red means stop, for everyone. If the light changes while the group is passing through, riders still approaching must stop and wait. The front group will ease up and let you rejoin. At roundabouts and unsignalled intersections, every rider checks independently. Do not follow the wheel in front through an intersection without looking yourself.

Bring what you need

Spare tube, tyre levers, pump or CO2, water, and food for the distance. Make sure your tyres are pumped, your chain is clean, and your brakes work before you leave the house. The group will wait for a puncture. The group should not have to wait because a bike was not ready to ride.

If you are new

Welcome. Sit towards the back of the group for your first few rides while you get comfortable with the pace and the calls. Let the ride leader know it is your first time so they can keep an eye out. There is zero expectation that you will take a turn on the front or know every signal on day one. Just ride smoothly, pay attention, and enjoy it. The rest will come.

These guidelines are here because group riding done well is a genuinely special thing. Everyone looking out for each other, moving together, sharing the effort and the road. That only works when everyone commits to riding predictably and generously. We take it seriously so that every Saturday can be relaxed, safe, and something worth coming back for.
